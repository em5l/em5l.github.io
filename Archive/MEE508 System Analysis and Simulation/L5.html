State-Space Formulation of Electrical Systems Using Lagrangian Method

1. System Description

Consider the two-loop RLC circuit shown below. The generalized coordinates, representing the mesh charges, are $q_1$ and $q_2$. The generalized velocities, representing the mesh currents, are $\dot{q}_1 = i_1$ and $\dot{q}_2 = i_2$.

  • Loop 1 contains a resistor R.
  • Loop 2 contains a capacitor C and a voltage source V.
  • An inductor L is in a branch common to both loops.

The system dynamics can be analyzed using the energy stored and dissipated within these components.

2. Energies and Generalized Forces

2.1 Magnetic (Kinetic) Energy

The inductor stores magnetic energy, which is treated as the system’s kinetic energy, T. It depends on the net current flowing through it, which is $i_L = i_1 - i_2 = \dot{q}_1 - \dot{q}_2$.

$$ T = E_I = \frac{1}{2} L (\dot{q}_1 - \dot{q}_2)^2 $$### 2.2 Electric (Potential) Energy

The capacitor stores electric potential energy, V. This energy depends on the charge accumulated on it, which is the mesh charge $q_2$.

$$V = E_A = \frac{1}{2C} q_2^2 $$### 2.3 Generalized Forces

The non-conservative elements (the voltage source and resistor) are accounted for by generalized forces, $Q_i$. These are derived from virtual work, $\delta W = \sum Q_i \delta q_i$.

  • The voltage source V does work on the charge in loop 2, so $Q_2 = V$.
  • The resistor R dissipates energy in loop 1. This can be represented as a dissipative force, $Q_1 = -R \dot{q}_1$.

3. Lagrangian Formulation

The Lagrangian of the system is the difference between its kinetic and potential energy:

$$ \mathcal{L} = T - V = \frac{1}{2} L (\dot{q}_1 - \dot{q}_2)^2 - \frac{1}{2C} q_2^2 $$### 3.1 Lagrange’s Equations

The equations of motion are derived using Lagrange’s equation for each generalized coordinate $q_i$:

$$\frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \right) - \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial q_i} = Q_i $$

For Coordinate $q_1$

The partial derivatives are:

$$ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{q}_1} = L(\dot{q}_1 - \dot{q}_2), \quad \frac{d}{dt} \left(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{q}_1}\right) = L(\ddot{q}_1 - \ddot{q}_2) $$

$$ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial q_1} = 0, \quad Q_1 = -R\dot{q}_1 $$

The resulting equation for the first loop is:

$$ L(\ddot{q}_1 - \ddot{q}_2) + R\dot{q}_1 = 0 $$

For Coordinate $q_2$

The partial derivatives are:

$$ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{q}_2} = -L(\dot{q}_1 - \dot{q}_2), \quad \frac{d}{dt} \left(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{q}_2}\right) = -L(\ddot{q}_1 - \ddot{q}_2) $$

$$ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial q_2} = -\frac{1}{C} q_2, \quad Q_2 = V $$

The resulting equation for the second loop is:

$$ -L(\ddot{q}_1 - \ddot{q}_2) + \frac{1}{C} q_2 = V $$

4. State-Space Model

The system has two independent energy storage elements (L and C), so it is a second-order system. We define a state vector $\mathbf{x}$ using physically meaningful variables: the current through the inductor ($i_L$) and the voltage across the capacitor ($v_C$).

Let the state variables be:

  • $x_1 = i_L = \dot{q}_1 - \dot{q}_2$
  • $x_2 = v_C = \frac{1}{C} q_2$

Now, we derive the state equations ($\dot{x}_1$ and $\dot{x}_2$) from the equations of motion.

Deriving the State Equations

  1. From the first loop’s equation, we can find an expression for $\dot{x}_1$: $$ L(\ddot{q}_1 - \ddot{q}_2) = -R\dot{q}_1 \implies L\dot{x}_1 = -R\dot{q}_1 $$

  2. By adding the two loop equations, we get an algebraic relation: $$ (L(\ddot{q}_1 - \ddot{q}_2) + R\dot{q}_1) + (-L(\ddot{q}_1 - \ddot{q}_2) + \frac{1}{C}q_2) = 0 + V $$ This simplifies to: $$ R\dot{q}_1 + \frac{1}{C}q_2 = V \implies R\dot{q}_1 + x_2 = V $$ Solving for $\dot{q}_1$ gives: $$ \dot{q}_1 = \frac{1}{R}(V - x_2) $$

  3. Substitute the expression for $\dot{q}_1$ back into the equation for $\dot{x}_1$: $$ L\dot{x}_1 = -R \left( \frac{1}{R}(V - x_2) \right) = -V + x_2 \implies \dot{x}_1 = \frac{1}{L}x_2 - \frac{1}{L}V $$

  4. To find $\dot{x}_2$, we use the definition $\dot{x}_2 = \frac{1}{C}\dot{q}_2$. We know $\dot{q}_2 = \dot{q}_1 - x_1$. Substituting the expression for $\dot{q}_1$: $$ \dot{q}_2 = \frac{1}{R}(V - x_2) - x_1 $$ Therefore, $\dot{x}_2$ is: $$ \dot{x}_2 = \frac{1}{C}\dot{q}_2 = \frac{1}{C} \left( -x_1 - \frac{1}{R}x_2 + \frac{1}{R}V \right) = -\frac{1}{C}x_1 - \frac{1}{RC}x_2 + \frac{1}{RC}V $$

Final State-Space Form

This gives the standard state-space form $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A \mathbf{x} + B u$, where the input is $u=V$:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}_1 \\ \dot{x}_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & \frac{1}{L} \\ -\frac{1}{C} & -\frac{1}{RC} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -\frac{1}{L} \\ \frac{1}{RC} \end{bmatrix} V $$
